Week 2: Teaching BERT Early Modern English — Fine-Tuning on the Geneva Bible

Overview

Last week, we saw that BERT produces powerful contextual embeddings — but we also saw its limits. When we gave BERT sentences from the Geneva Bible (1560), its masked-word predictions were sometimes off, and its confidence was lower than for modern English. This is because BERT was pre-trained on Wikipedia and contemporary books: it knows modern English fluently, but Early Modern English is a foreign dialect.

This week, we fix that. We will fine-tune BERT on the Geneva Bible using Masked Language Modeling (MLM) — the same task BERT was originally trained on. The idea is simple: by continuing to train BERT on 16th-century text, we teach it the spelling, vocabulary, and syntax of the period. This process is called domain-adaptive pretraining.

By the end of this tutorial, you will be able to:

  1. Load and prepare a historical corpus for language model fine-tuning
  2. Evaluate BERT’s baseline performance on Early Modern English
  3. Fine-tune BERT using masked language modeling
  4. Measure whether the fine-tuned model actually improved
  5. Save a domain-adapted model for use on other historical texts (next week)

Part I — Conceptual Background: Why Fine-Tune?

The Problem

BERT’s pre-training corpus was modern. When it encounters “heauen” (heaven), “darkenesse” (darkness), “vnto” (unto), or “saide” (said), these words either:

  • Get split into unusual subword pieces (e.g., “heauen” → ["he", "##au", "##en"])
  • Get misinterpreted because the surrounding syntax is unfamiliar

The embeddings BERT produces for Early Modern sentences are therefore noisier and less semantically coherent than those it produces for modern text.

The Solution

We continue BERT’s MLM training on a large body of Early Modern English. The Geneva Bible is ideal for this because:

  • It is large (~31,000 verses), giving the model plenty of data
  • It is clean, well-structured text (no OCR errors)
  • It is saturated with the orthography and syntax of the 1560s
  • Its vocabulary overlaps heavily with the mercantile documents we will analyze next week

After fine-tuning, BERT will produce better embeddings for any Early Modern English text — not just biblical text. The model learns the language, not just the content.

What MLM Fine-Tuning Actually Does

During fine-tuning, we randomly mask 15% of tokens in each sentence and ask BERT to predict them. When BERT predicts “heauen” where “heauen” was masked, it updates its internal weights to be better at representing that word and its context. Over thousands of such predictions across the whole Geneva Bible, BERT gradually learns:

  • That “heauen” means heaven
  • That “saide” functions like “said”
  • That “the Lorde God” is a common phrase in this register
  • That “and” often appears in long coordinate structures (a hallmark of biblical prose)

Noticing the Language

Take a moment to read these verses. Notice:

  • Spelling: “heauen” for heaven, “darkenesse” for darkness, “vnto” for unto, “sayde” for said
  • Verb forms: “hath,” “doth,” “saith” — archaic conjugations
  • Pronouns: “thee,” “thy,” “ye” — the familiar second person
  • Syntax: frequent inversion (“Then saide the Lord”), long coordinate clauses with “and”

These are exactly the features that standard BERT struggles with, and exactly what fine-tuning will teach it.

Part IV — Baseline Evaluation: How Well Does BERT Know This Language?

Before fine-tuning, let’s measure BERT’s current ability with Early Modern English. We will do two things: test MLM predictions and compute embedding quality.

Baseline MLM Predictions

```{python}
from transformers import pipeline

# Load the base (un-fine-tuned) BERT for masked language modeling
fill_mask = pipeline("fill-mask", model="bert-base-uncased", device=-1)

# Select test sentences — we will use the SAME ones after fine-tuning
baseline_tests = [
    ("And God sawe the [MASK] that it was good.", "light"),
    ("In the beginning God created the [MASK] and the earth.", "heauen"),
    ("The Lord God also made the man of the [MASK] of the grounde.", "dust"),
    ("And the serpent saide vnto the [MASK], Ye shall not die.", "woman"),
    ("And God [MASK] them, saying, Bring foorth fruite and multiplie.", "blessed"),
    ("The Lord is my [MASK], I shall not want.", "shepherd"),
    ("Thou shalt not [MASK]: thou shalt not commit adulterie.", "kill"),
    ("And God called the dry land, [MASK], and he called the gathering together of the waters, Seas.", "earth"),
]

print("=== BASELINE MLM Predictions (Before Fine-Tuning) ===\n")
baseline_results = []

for masked_sent, expected in baseline_tests:
    predictions = fill_mask(masked_sent)
    top_tokens = [p["token_str"] for p in predictions[:5]]
    top_scores = [p["score"] for p in predictions[:5]]
    
    hit = expected.lower() in [t.lower() for t in top_tokens]
    baseline_results.append({
        "sentence": masked_sent,
        "expected": expected,
        "top_prediction": top_tokens[0],
        "top_score": top_scores[0],
        "in_top_5": hit,
        "all_predictions": top_tokens
    })
    
    status = "✓" if hit else "✗"
    print(f"{status} Expected: {expected:12s} | Top prediction: {top_tokens[0]:12s} "
          f"(score: {top_scores[0]:.3f})")
    print(f"  Top 5: {top_tokens}")
    print(f"  Sentence: {masked_sent}\n")

baseline_accuracy = sum(1 for r in baseline_results if r["in_top_5"]) / len(baseline_results)
baseline_avg_score = np.mean([r["top_score"] for r in baseline_results])
print(f"Baseline: {baseline_accuracy:.0%} of expected words in top 5")
print(f"Average top-prediction confidence: {baseline_avg_score:.3f}")
```

Note these numbers — we will compare them directly after fine-tuning.

Baseline Embedding Quality

We also want to measure whether BERT produces coherent embeddings for Geneva Bible text. We will compute similarity between theologically related verses and unrelated verses.

```{python}
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity

st_model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")

# Pairs of related verses
related_pairs = [
    ("In the beginning God created the heauen and the earth.",
     "Thus the heauens and the earth were finished, and all the host of them."),
    ("And God saide, Let there be light: And there was light.",
     "And God called the light, Day, and the darkenes, he called Night."),
    ("The Lord is my shepherd, I shall not want.",
     "He maketh me to rest in greene pasture, and leadeth me by the still waters."),
]

# Pairs of unrelated verses
unrelated_pairs = [
    ("In the beginning God created the heauen and the earth.",
     "Thou shalt not kill: thou shalt not commit adulterie."),
    ("And God saide, Let there be light: And there was light.",
     "The Lord is my shepherd, I shall not want."),
    ("And the serpent saide vnto the woman, Ye shall not die.",
     "He maketh me to rest in greene pasture, and leadeth me by the still waters."),
]

print("=== Baseline Embedding Similarity ===\n")

print("Related pairs (should be HIGH similarity):")
related_sims = []
for a, b in related_pairs:
    embs = st_model.encode([a, b])
    sim = cosine_similarity([embs[0]], [embs[1]])[0][0]
    related_sims.append(sim)
    print(f"  {sim:.3f}  |  {a[:50]}...")

print(f"\n  Average: {np.mean(related_sims):.3f}\n")

print("Unrelated pairs (should be LOW similarity):")
unrelated_sims = []
for a, b in unrelated_pairs:
    embs = st_model.encode([a, b])
    sim = cosine_similarity([embs[0]], [embs[1]])[0][0]
    unrelated_sims.append(sim)
    print(f"  {sim:.3f}  |  {a[:50]}...")

print(f"\n  Average: {np.mean(unrelated_sims):.3f}")
print(f"\n  Gap (related - unrelated): {np.mean(related_sims) - np.mean(unrelated_sims):.3f}")
```

A larger gap between related and unrelated scores means BERT is doing a better job distinguishing semantically connected verses from unconnected ones.

Part V — Preparing the Geneva Bible for Fine-Tuning

Now we prepare the data for training. We need to:

  1. Extract the text of each verse
  2. Group verses into longer chunks (individual verses are often too short for effective training)
  3. Tokenize everything with BERT’s tokenizer
```{python}
# Load the tokenizer
tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")

# Clean and extract text
texts = df["Text"].dropna().tolist()
print(f"Total verses with text: {len(texts):,}")

# Show tokenization of a sample verse
sample = "And the earth was without forme and voide, and darkenesse was vpon the deepe."
tokens = tokenizer.tokenize(sample)
print(f"\nSample verse: {sample}")
print(f"Tokens ({len(tokens)}): {tokens}")
```

Notice how BERT’s tokenizer splits archaic words into subword pieces. “Darkenesse” might become ["dark", "##ness", "##e"] and “voide” might become ["void", "##e"]. The tokenizer does its best, but these unusual splits are part of why base BERT struggles with this text.

```{python}
# Group verses into chapter-level chunks for better training context
# Individual verses are too short; chapters give BERT more context to learn from

chapters = df.groupby(["Book Name", "Chapter"])["Text"].apply(
    lambda x: " ".join(x.dropna())
).tolist()

print(f"Number of chapter-level chunks: {len(chapters)}")
print(f"Average chunk length (characters): {np.mean([len(c) for c in chapters]):.0f}")
print(f"Average chunk length (words): {np.mean([len(c.split()) for c in chapters]):.0f}")

# Show a sample chunk
print(f"\nSample chapter chunk (first 300 chars):")
print(chapters[0][:300] + "...")
```
```{python}
# Tokenize all chunks
# We truncate to 512 tokens (BERT's maximum) and allow the tokenizer to 
# handle chunks longer than 512 by truncation.

def tokenize_function(examples):
    return tokenizer(
        examples["text"], 
        truncation=True, 
        max_length=512,
        padding="max_length",
        return_special_tokens_mask=True
    )

# Create a HuggingFace Dataset
dataset = Dataset.from_dict({"text": chapters})
print(f"Dataset size: {len(dataset)} chunks")

# Tokenize
tokenized_dataset = dataset.map(
    tokenize_function, 
    batched=True, 
    remove_columns=["text"],
    desc="Tokenizing"
)

print(f"Tokenized dataset: {tokenized_dataset}")
print(f"Features: {list(tokenized_dataset.features.keys())}")
```
```{python}
# Split into train and evaluation sets (90/10)
split = tokenized_dataset.train_test_split(test_size=0.1, seed=42)

print(f"Training chunks: {len(train_dataset)}")
```

Part VI — Fine-Tuning BERT

Now we train. This is the core of the tutorial. We load BERT’s masked language model head, set up a data collator that handles the random masking, and train for a few epochs.

Understanding the Training Loop

Here is what happens at each step:

  1. The data collator randomly masks 15% of tokens in each chunk
  2. The masked chunk is fed through BERT
  3. BERT predicts what the masked tokens should be
  4. The loss measures how wrong those predictions were
  5. The optimizer updates BERT’s weights to reduce the loss
  6. Repeat for every chunk, for several passes (epochs) through the data
```{python}
# Load BERT with the MLM head (the prediction layer on top)
model = BertForMaskedLM.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model.to(device)

print(f"Model parameters: {model.num_parameters():,}")
print(f"Model is on: {next(model.parameters()).device}")
```
```{python}
# The data collator handles random masking automatically
# mlm_probability=0.15 means 15% of tokens get masked each time
data_collator = DataCollatorForLanguageModeling(
    tokenizer=tokenizer,
    mlm=True,
    mlm_probability=0.15
)

# Training configuration
training_args = TrainingArguments(
    output_dir="./geneva_bert_checkpoints",
    overwrite_output_dir=True,
    
    # Training hyperparameters
    num_train_epochs=3,               # 3 passes through the data
    per_device_train_batch_size=8,    # Process 8 chunks at a time
    learning_rate=2e-5,               # Small learning rate (standard for fine-tuning)
    weight_decay=0.01,                # Regularization to prevent overfitting
    warmup_steps=100,                 # Gradually increase learning rate at start
    
    # Evaluation
    eval_strategy="epoch",            # Evaluate after each epoch
    
    # Logging
    logging_steps=50,                 # Log loss every 50 steps
    logging_dir="./logs",
    
    # Saving
    save_strategy="epoch",
    save_total_limit=2,               # Keep only the 2 best checkpoints
    
    # Performance
    fp16=torch.cuda.is_available(),   # Use half-precision if GPU available
    dataloader_num_workers=0,
    
    # Disable wandb/mlflow
    report_to="none",
)

print("Training configuration set.")
print(f"  Epochs: {training_args.num_train_epochs}")
print(f"  Batch size: {training_args.per_device_train_batch_size}")
print(f"  Learning rate: {training_args.learning_rate}")
print(f"  Warmup steps: {training_args.warmup_steps}")
```
```{python}
# Create the Trainer
trainer = Trainer(
    model=model,
    args=training_args,
    train_dataset=train_dataset,
    eval_dataset=eval_dataset,
    data_collator=data_collator,
)

print("Trainer ready. Starting fine-tuning...\n")
print("=" * 60)
print("NOTE: This will take 10-30 minutes on CPU, or 2-5 minutes on GPU.")
print("Watch the loss decrease over time — that means BERT is learning.")
print("=" * 60)
```
```{python}
# Train!
train_result = trainer.train()

print("\n=== Training Complete ===")
print(f"Total training time: {train_result.metrics['train_runtime']:.0f} seconds")
print(f"Final training loss: {train_result.metrics['train_loss']:.4f}")
```
```{python}
# Evaluate on held-out data
eval_results = trainer.evaluate()

print("\n=== Evaluation Results ===")
print(f"Eval loss: {eval_results['eval_loss']:.4f}")
print(f"Perplexity: {np.exp(eval_results['eval_loss']):.2f}")
```

Perplexity is a common metric for language models. It roughly measures “how surprised the model is by the text.” Lower is better. A perplexity of 10 means the model is, on average, choosing between 10 equally likely options for each masked token. After fine-tuning on the Geneva Bible, this should be noticeably lower than before — BERT is less “surprised” by Early Modern English.

Part VIII — Post Fine-Tuning Evaluation

Now the key question: did fine-tuning actually help? Let’s re-run the exact same tests from Part IV.

MLM Predictions: Before vs. After

```{python}
# Load the fine-tuned model for fill-mask
fill_mask_ft = pipeline("fill-mask", model=save_path, tokenizer=save_path, device=-1)

print("=== POST FINE-TUNING MLM Predictions ===\n")
print(f"{'Expected':<12} | {'BEFORE':^30} | {'AFTER':^30} | Improved?")
print("-" * 90)

finetuned_results = []

for i, (masked_sent, expected) in enumerate(baseline_tests):
    # Fine-tuned predictions
    predictions_ft = fill_mask_ft(masked_sent)
    top_tokens_ft = [p["token_str"] for p in predictions_ft[:5]]
    top_score_ft = predictions_ft[0]["score"]
    hit_ft = expected.lower() in [t.lower() for t in top_tokens_ft]
    
    finetuned_results.append({
        "expected": expected,
        "top_prediction_ft": top_tokens_ft[0],
        "top_score_ft": top_score_ft,
        "in_top_5_ft": hit_ft,
    })
    
    # Compare with baseline
    b = baseline_results[i]
    before_str = f"{b['top_prediction']:>12s} ({b['top_score']:.3f})"
    after_str = f"{top_tokens_ft[0]:>12s} ({top_score_ft:.3f})"
    
    improved = ""
    if hit_ft and not b["in_top_5"]:
        improved = "✓ NEW HIT"
    elif hit_ft and b["in_top_5"]:
        if top_score_ft > b["top_score"]:
            improved = "↑ higher confidence"
        else:
            improved = "= still correct"
    elif not hit_ft and b["in_top_5"]:
        improved = "↓ regression"
    else:
        improved = "— still missed"
    
    print(f"{expected:<12} | {before_str:>30} | {after_str:>30} | {improved}")

ft_accuracy = sum(1 for r in finetuned_results if r["in_top_5_ft"]) / len(finetuned_results)
ft_avg_score = np.mean([r["top_score_ft"] for r in finetuned_results])

print(f"\nBaseline accuracy (top 5): {baseline_accuracy:.0%}")
print(f"Fine-tuned accuracy (top 5): {ft_accuracy:.0%}")
print(f"\nBaseline avg confidence: {baseline_avg_score:.3f}")
print(f"Fine-tuned avg confidence: {ft_avg_score:.3f}")
```

Part IX — Visualizing the Training Process

Let’s look at how the training loss changed over time. A decreasing loss means the model was progressively getting better at predicting masked tokens in Geneva Bible text.

```{python}
# Extract training loss history from the trainer
log_history = trainer.state.log_history

# Separate training and eval logs
train_logs = [l for l in log_history if "loss" in l and "eval_loss" not in l]
eval_logs = [l for l in log_history if "eval_loss" in l]

fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

# Training loss over steps
if train_logs:
    steps = [l["step"] for l in train_logs]
    losses = [l["loss"] for l in train_logs]
    ax1.plot(steps, losses, "b-", alpha=0.7, linewidth=1.5)
    ax1.set_xlabel("Training Step")
    ax1.set_ylabel("Loss")
    ax1.set_title("Training Loss Over Time")
    ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Eval loss per epoch
if eval_logs:
    epochs = list(range(1, len(eval_logs) + 1))
    eval_losses = [l["eval_loss"] for l in eval_logs]
    ax2.bar(epochs, eval_losses, color="steelblue", edgecolor="black", alpha=0.8)
    ax2.set_xlabel("Epoch")
    ax2.set_ylabel("Evaluation Loss")
    ax2.set_title("Evaluation Loss Per Epoch")
    
    for e, l in zip(epochs, eval_losses):
        ax2.text(e, l + 0.02, f"{l:.3f}", ha="center", fontsize=10)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("training_progress.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight")
plt.show()
```

What to look for

  • Training loss should decrease over time, showing that the model is learning
  • Evaluation loss should also decrease (or at least not increase much), showing that the model is generalizing rather than just memorizing

If evaluation loss starts increasing while training loss keeps dropping, that would be a sign of overfitting — the model is memorizing the training data rather than learning general patterns. With 3 epochs on a dataset this size, overfitting is unlikely, but it is worth checking.

Part X — What Changed Inside the Model?

To build intuition, let’s look at how the fine-tuned model’s internal representations changed for specific Early Modern English words.

```{python}
from transformers import BertModel

# Load both models
base_model = BertModel.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
ft_model = BertModel.from_pretrained(save_path)
base_model.eval()
ft_model.eval()

test_sentences = [
    "And the earth was without forme and voide.",
    "The Lorde God made the heauens and the earth.",
    "And God saide vnto them, be fruitful and multiplie.",
    "For God so loued the world that he gaue his onely begotten Sonne.",
]

print("=== Embedding Shift After Fine-Tuning ===\n")
print("Cosine similarity between base and fine-tuned [CLS] embeddings:\n")

for sent in test_sentences:
    inputs = tokenizer(sent, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=512)
    
    with torch.no_grad():
        base_out = base_model(**inputs).last_hidden_state[0, 0, :].numpy()
        ft_out = ft_model(**inputs).last_hidden_state[0, 0, :].numpy()
    
    sim = cosine_similarity([base_out], [ft_out])[0][0]
    print(f"  {sim:.4f}  |  {sent[:60]}...")

print("\nValues below ~0.95 indicate the model has meaningfully adjusted")
print("its representation of the sentence after fine-tuning.")
```

Part XI — A Look Ahead: Early Modern English Beyond the Bible

The fine-tuned model now understands Geneva Bible language. But does that knowledge transfer to other Early Modern texts? Let’s test it briefly with some non-biblical sentences:

```{python}
print("=== Transfer Test: Non-Biblical Early Modern English ===\n")

mercantile_tests = [
    "The [MASK] of London did trade in silks and spices.",
    "The companie did advance [MASK] beyond the seas.",
    "By the kings [MASK] the merchants were granted free passage.",
    "The balance of [MASK] was much debated in Parliament.",
]

for sent in mercantile_tests:
    pred_base = fill_mask(sent)
    pred_ft = fill_mask_ft(sent)
    
    print(f"Sentence: {sent}")
    print(f"  Base BERT:     {[p['token_str'] for p in pred_base[:3]]}")
    print(f"  Fine-tuned:    {[p['token_str'] for p in pred_ft[:3]]}")
    print()
```

Even though these sentences were not in the training data (they are about commerce, not scripture), the fine-tuned model should produce more period-appropriate predictions. This is the transferability of domain adaptation: the model learned the language of the period, not just the content of the Bible.
