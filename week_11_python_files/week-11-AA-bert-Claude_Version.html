Week 12: What Does BERT ‘Know’ About Mercantilism? Probing Embeddings Across Centuries

Week 12 goals:

In Week 10, we ended with an important insight: spaCy’s NER model works by building contextual token embeddings—numerical representations that capture not just a word’s meaning in isolation, but its meaning in context. The example we used was “Apple”: the word gets a different embedding in “Apple released a new phone” versus “I ate an apple.”

This week, we are going to take that insight further and ask a harder question: what happens when the context is not just a different sentence, but a different century?

We will work with BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers), a model that builds contextual embeddings using a transformer architecture. If you have used spaCy’s transformer-based pipelines (en_core_web_trf), you have already used something similar under the hood. The key difference from spaCy’s default CNN-based embeddings is that BERT uses self-attention: every token’s representation is informed by every other token in the sentence simultaneously, not just a local window.

Our plan is to:

  1. Extract BERT embeddings for key mercantilist terms from our Early Modern texts
  2. Extract BERT embeddings for the same words from modern economics texts
  3. Visualize the embedding space using dimensionality reduction (PCA and t-SNE)
  4. Interpret what we find, and discuss the implications for NER on historical corpora

Why this matters for NER: if BERT was pre-trained on modern internet text (Wikipedia, BooksCorpus), then its contextual representations of words in 17th-century prose may be systematically different from what it “expects.” This has direct consequences for how well NER models built on BERT will perform on historical documents—which is exactly what the Ehrmann et al. survey we discussed warns about.

Big picture workflow

  1. Load historical texts from the week_12_texts/ folder
  2. Extract sentences containing our target words
  3. Build a small comparison corpus of modern economics sentences
  4. Load a pre-trained BERT model (from Hugging Face)
  5. Extract contextual embeddings for each target word in each sentence
  6. Reduce dimensionality with PCA and t-SNE
  7. Visualize and interpret the embedding space
  8. Discuss what this means for NER on historical documents

Step 1: Load Historical Texts and Extract Sentences

We need to pull out sentences from our Early Modern corpus that contain specific target words. These are the words whose contextual embeddings we want to compare across centuries.

Choosing target words

Our target words are terms that appear in mercantilist texts and in modern economics, but whose meanings have shifted over time. Here is our list, with brief notes on why each is interesting:

  • “trade” — core concept in both periods; likely to show some overlap but different connotations (bilateral exchange vs. abstract economic policy)
  • “balance” — in mercantilist texts, almost always “balance of trade” (a specific doctrine about gold flows); in modern economics, broader usage
  • “manufacture” — etymologically “making by hand” (Latin manu factus); in Early Modern texts, closer to this artisanal meaning; in modern usage, industrial mass production
  • “stock” — in mercantilist texts, often means goods, inventory, or even livestock; in modern texts, usually financial shares or capital stock
  • “interest” — mercantilist usage often means political advantage or national concern; modern usage skews toward financial interest rates
  • “commerce” — used in both periods, but with different institutional assumptions
  • “plantation” — in Early Modern texts, often means a colonial settlement or the act of planting/establishing; modern usage is narrower

Create a file step1_extract_historical_sentences.py and run it:

from pathlib import Path
import nltk
import json
import re

nltk.download("punkt", quiet=True)
nltk.download("punkt_tab", quiet=True)

TEXT_DIR = Path("week_12_texts")
txt_paths = sorted(TEXT_DIR.glob("*.txt"))

print(f"Found {len(txt_paths)} text files.")

# Our target words
TARGET_WORDS = ["trade", "balance", "manufacture", "stock", "interest", "commerce", "plantation"]

# We will store results as a list of dictionaries
results = []

for path in txt_paths:
    with open(path, "r", encoding="utf-8", errors="ignore") as f:
        text = f.read()

    sentences = nltk.sent_tokenize(text)

    for sent in sentences:
        # Clean up whitespace (Early Modern texts can have odd formatting)
        sent_clean = re.sub(r'\s+', ' ', sent).strip()

        # Skip very short or very long sentences
        word_count = len(sent_clean.split())
        if word_count < 5 or word_count > 80:
            continue

        sent_lower = sent_clean.lower()

        for target in TARGET_WORDS:
            # Match whole words only
            if re.search(r'\b' + target + r'\b', sent_lower):
                results.append({
                    "sentence": sent_clean,
                    "target_word": target,
                    "source": "historical",
                    "file": path.name
                })

print(f"\nTotal sentence-word pairs extracted: {len(results)}")
print("\nBreakdown by target word:")
for word in TARGET_WORDS:
    count = sum(1 for r in results if r["target_word"] == word)
    print(f"  {word}: {count}")

# Save
Path("data").mkdir(exist_ok=True)
with open(Path("data") / "historical_sentences.json", "w", encoding="utf-8") as f:
    json.dump(results, f, ensure_ascii=False, indent=2)

print("\nSaved to data/historical_sentences.json")

You will likely see that some words (like “trade”) appear in thousands of sentences, while others (like “plantation”) may appear in far fewer. That is fine—we will sample down later. For now, we want to capture everything.

Step 4: Extract BERT Embeddings

This is the conceptual core of the tutorial. We are going to take each sentence, pass it through BERT, and extract the embedding vector for our target word in that specific sentence context. This is the same principle from Week 10—contextual embeddings—but now we are using a much more powerful model.

What BERT does

When you pass a sentence through BERT:

  1. The tokenizer breaks the sentence into subword tokens. BERT uses WordPiece tokenization, so a word like “manufacture” might stay as one token, but “merchandizing” could become ["merchant", "##izing"]. This is different from spaCy’s tokenization and you should be aware of it.

  2. The tokens pass through 12 transformer layers (in bert-base). Each layer applies self-attention: every token “looks at” every other token to build up its representation.

  3. The output is a tensor of shape (1, sequence_length, 768)—one 768-dimensional vector for each token in the sentence.

  4. We extract the vector(s) corresponding to our target word. If the word was split into multiple subword tokens, we average their vectors.

The result: a 768-dimensional vector that represents what “stock” means in this particular sentence. A different sentence will give a different vector for the same word.

Create a file step4_extract_embeddings.py. Read the code and the explanations before running it—this step will take a few minutes.

import json
import re
from pathlib import Path

import torch
from transformers import BertTokenizer, BertModel

DATA_DIR = Path("data")

with open(DATA_DIR / "sampled_sentences.json", "r", encoding="utf-8") as f:
    sampled = json.load(f)

print(f"Loaded {len(sampled)} sentence-word pairs.")

# Load BERT
print("Loading BERT model...")
tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model = BertModel.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model.eval()  # Set to evaluation mode (no dropout)

def get_word_embedding(sentence, target_word, tokenizer, model):
    """
    Extract the contextual embedding for a target word in a sentence.

    Steps:
    1. Tokenize the sentence with BERT's tokenizer
    2. Find which token(s) correspond to our target word
    3. Run the sentence through BERT
    4. Extract and average the hidden states for those tokens

    Returns a 768-dimensional vector, or None if the word isn't found.
    """
    # Tokenize
    inputs = tokenizer(sentence, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=512)

    # Get token-to-word mapping
    # BERT adds [CLS] at the start and [SEP] at the end
    tokens = tokenizer.convert_ids_to_tokens(inputs["input_ids"][0])

    # Find the target word's token positions
    # We need to handle subword tokenization: "manufacture" might be
    # ["man", "##uf", "##act", "##ure"] in BERT's vocabulary
    target_lower = target_word.lower()

    # Strategy: reconstruct words from subword tokens and find the match
    target_indices = []
    current_word = ""
    current_indices = []

    for i, token in enumerate(tokens):
        if token in ["[CLS]", "[SEP]", "[PAD]"]:
            # Check if the word we just finished is our target
            if current_word == target_lower and current_indices:
                target_indices = current_indices
                break
            current_word = ""
            current_indices = []
            continue

        if token.startswith("##"):
            # Continuation of previous word
            current_word += token[2:]
            current_indices.append(i)
        else:
            # Check previous word before starting new one
            if current_word == target_lower and current_indices:
                target_indices = current_indices
                break
            # Start new word
            current_word = token
            current_indices = [i]

    # Check the last word
    if not target_indices and current_word == target_lower and current_indices:
        target_indices = current_indices

    if not target_indices:
        return None

    # Run through BERT
    with torch.no_grad():
        outputs = model(**inputs)

    # outputs.last_hidden_state has shape (1, seq_len, 768)
    hidden_states = outputs.last_hidden_state[0]  # Remove batch dimension

    # Average the hidden states for the target word's tokens
    word_embedding = hidden_states[target_indices].mean(dim=0)

    return word_embedding.numpy()


# Extract embeddings for all sentences
print("Extracting embeddings (this may take a few minutes on CPU)...")

embeddings_data = []
skipped = 0

for i, item in enumerate(sampled):
    emb = get_word_embedding(item["sentence"], item["target_word"], tokenizer, model)

    if emb is not None:
        embeddings_data.append({
            "embedding": emb.tolist(),
            "target_word": item["target_word"],
            "source": item["source"],
            "sentence": item["sentence"]
        })
    else:
        skipped += 1

    # Progress indicator
    if (i + 1) % 25 == 0:
        print(f"  Processed {i + 1}/{len(sampled)} sentences...")

print(f"\nDone. Extracted {len(embeddings_data)} embeddings, skipped {skipped}.")

# Save
with open(DATA_DIR / "bert_embeddings.json", "w", encoding="utf-8") as f:
    json.dump(embeddings_data, f)

print("Saved to data/bert_embeddings.json")

A few things to note as you watch this run:

  • model.eval(): we set BERT to evaluation mode. This turns off dropout (random neuron deactivation), which is only needed during training. Without this, you would get slightly different embeddings each time you run the same sentence.

  • torch.no_grad(): this tells PyTorch not to track gradients, which saves memory and speeds things up. We are not training anything—just extracting representations.

  • Subword handling: the get_word_embedding function carefully reconstructs words from BERT’s subword tokens. This is important because if “manufacture” is split into ["man", "##uf", "##act", "##ure"], we need to average across all four token vectors to get a single word-level embedding.

  • Skipped sentences: some sentences may be skipped if the target word is not found after tokenization (this can happen with very unusual Early Modern spellings). A few skips are normal.

Step 5: Visualize with PCA

We now have a collection of 768-dimensional vectors. Each one represents a word-in-context. We cannot visualize 768 dimensions directly, so we use dimensionality reduction to project them down to 2D.

We will start with PCA (Principal Component Analysis), which finds the directions of maximum variance in the data and projects onto them. PCA is linear and deterministic—you will get the same result every time.

Create a file step5_visualize_pca.py and run it:

import json
import numpy as np
from pathlib import Path

from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib

matplotlib.rcParams['figure.dpi'] = 150

DATA_DIR = Path("data")

with open(DATA_DIR / "bert_embeddings.json", "r", encoding="utf-8") as f:
    data = json.load(f)

print(f"Loaded {len(data)} embeddings.")

# Extract the embedding matrix and metadata
embeddings = np.array([d["embedding"] for d in data])
words = [d["target_word"] for d in data]
sources = [d["source"] for d in data]

print(f"Embedding matrix shape: {embeddings.shape}")

# Fit PCA
pca = PCA(n_components=2)
reduced = pca.fit_transform(embeddings)

print(f"Explained variance: PC1={pca.explained_variance_ratio_[0]:.3f}, PC2={pca.explained_variance_ratio_[1]:.3f}")
print(f"Total explained: {sum(pca.explained_variance_ratio_):.3f}")

# Plot
TARGET_WORDS = ["trade", "balance", "manufacture", "stock", "interest", "commerce", "plantation"]

# Color map: one color per word
colors = plt.cm.tab10(np.linspace(0, 1, len(TARGET_WORDS)))
word_to_color = {w: colors[i] for i, w in enumerate(TARGET_WORDS)}

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 9))

for i in range(len(reduced)):
    color = word_to_color[words[i]]
    marker = "o" if sources[i] == "historical" else "^"  # circle vs triangle
    alpha = 0.6 if sources[i] == "historical" else 0.9
    edgecolor = "black" if sources[i] == "modern" else "none"
    ax.scatter(reduced[i, 0], reduced[i, 1],
               c=[color], marker=marker, s=60,
               alpha=alpha, edgecolors=edgecolor, linewidths=0.5)

# Legend for words
for word in TARGET_WORDS:
    ax.scatter([], [], c=[word_to_color[word]], label=word, s=60)

# Legend for sources
ax.scatter([], [], c="gray", marker="o", label="Historical", s=60)
ax.scatter([], [], c="gray", marker="^", label="Modern", s=60, edgecolors="black", linewidths=0.5)

ax.legend(loc="upper right", fontsize=8, ncol=2)
ax.set_xlabel(f"PC1 ({pca.explained_variance_ratio_[0]:.1%} variance)")
ax.set_ylabel(f"PC2 ({pca.explained_variance_ratio_[1]:.1%} variance)")
ax.set_title("BERT Contextual Embeddings: Historical vs. Modern Usage\n(PCA Projection)")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("pca_embeddings.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight")
plt.show()

print("\nSaved plot to pca_embeddings.png")

Reading the PCA plot

What you are looking for:

  • Circles (○) are historical uses; triangles (△) are modern uses.
  • Each color is a different target word.
  • If historical and modern uses of the same word cluster together, it means BERT represents them similarly despite the centuries between them. The word’s meaning (in BERT’s view) has not shifted much.
  • If they separate, it means BERT is picking up on contextual differences—the surrounding words in historical vs. modern sentences are pushing the embeddings apart.
  • Words like “manufacture” and “stock” should show noticeable separation. “Trade” may cluster more tightly.

Important caveat: PCA captures the directions of maximum variance, which may or may not align with the historical-vs-modern distinction. If the biggest source of variance is word identity (which it likely is—“trade” embeddings are far from “stock” embeddings), PCA will show that first. The historical/modern differences may be subtler and appear within word clusters.

Reading the t-SNE plot

t-SNE tends to produce more distinct clusters. Look for:

  • Do historical and modern uses of the same word form sub-clusters within the word’s region?

Step 7: Per-Word Focused Analysis

The global plots give us an overview, but to really see the historical/modern divergence, it helps to look at individual words. Let’s make a plot that shows, for each target word separately, how far apart its historical and modern embeddings are.

Create a file step7_per_word_analysis.py and run it:

import json
import numpy as np
from pathlib import Path
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib

matplotlib.rcParams['figure.dpi'] = 150

DATA_DIR = Path("data")

with open(DATA_DIR / "bert_embeddings.json", "r", encoding="utf-8") as f:
    data = json.load(f)

TARGET_WORDS = ["trade", "balance", "manufacture", "stock", "interest", "commerce", "plantation"]

print("Average cosine similarity between historical and modern embeddings:\n")

results = []

for word in TARGET_WORDS:
    hist_embs = [d["embedding"] for d in data if d["target_word"] == word and d["source"] == "historical"]
    mod_embs = [d["embedding"] for d in data if d["target_word"] == word and d["source"] == "modern"]

    if not hist_embs or not mod_embs:
        print(f"  {word}: insufficient data (hist={len(hist_embs)}, mod={len(mod_embs)})")
        continue

    hist_embs = np.array(hist_embs)
    mod_embs = np.array(mod_embs)

    # Average embedding for each group
    hist_centroid = hist_embs.mean(axis=0, keepdims=True)
    mod_centroid = mod_embs.mean(axis=0, keepdims=True)

    # Cosine similarity between centroids
    sim = cosine_similarity(hist_centroid, mod_centroid)[0][0]

    # Also compute within-group similarity for reference
    if len(hist_embs) > 1:
        hist_internal = cosine_similarity(hist_embs).mean()
    else:
        hist_internal = 1.0

    if len(mod_embs) > 1:
        mod_internal = cosine_similarity(mod_embs).mean()
    else:
        mod_internal = 1.0

    results.append({
        "word": word,
        "cross_similarity": sim,
        "hist_internal": hist_internal,
        "mod_internal": mod_internal,
        "n_hist": len(hist_embs),
        "n_mod": len(mod_embs)
    })

    print(f"  {word:15s}  cross={sim:.3f}  hist_internal={hist_internal:.3f}  mod_internal={mod_internal:.3f}  (n={len(hist_embs)}h, {len(mod_embs)}m)")

# Bar chart: cross-group similarity
print("\n--- Interpretation guide ---")
print("Higher cross-similarity → BERT sees historical and modern usage as more similar")
print("Lower cross-similarity → BERT sees a bigger gap between centuries")
print("Compare cross-similarity to internal similarity to see if the gap is meaningful")

words_plot = [r["word"] for r in results]
cross_sims = [r["cross_similarity"] for r in results]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
bars = ax.bar(words_plot, cross_sims, color="steelblue", edgecolor="black", alpha=0.8)

ax.set_ylabel("Cosine Similarity (Historical ↔ Modern Centroid)")
ax.set_xlabel("Target Word")
ax.set_title("How Similarly Does BERT Represent Historical vs. Modern Usage?")
ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
ax.axhline(y=0.9, color="red", linestyle="--", alpha=0.5, label="High similarity threshold")
ax.legend()

# Add value labels on bars
for bar, val in zip(bars, cross_sims):
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width()/2, bar.get_height() + 0.01,
            f"{val:.3f}", ha="center", va="bottom", fontsize=9)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("per_word_similarity.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight")
plt.show()

print("\nSaved plot to per_word_similarity.png")

What to look for

  • Words with high cross-similarity (close to 1.0) → BERT treats historical and modern usage as essentially the same. These words have stable meanings across centuries.
  • Words with lower cross-similarity → BERT detects a meaningful difference. These are the words where historical meaning diverges from modern meaning.
  • Compare cross-similarity to internal similarity. If historical sentences for “stock” have an internal similarity of 0.85, but cross-similarity with modern is 0.60, then the historical uses are more similar to each other than to the modern uses—a clear signal of semantic shift.
